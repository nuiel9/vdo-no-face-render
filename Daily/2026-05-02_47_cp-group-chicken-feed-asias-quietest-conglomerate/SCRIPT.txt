In 1921, two brothers from Guangdong arrived in Bangkok and opened a seed shop in Yaowarat, the city's Chinatown district. They had no local connections, no outside capital, and no guarantee of survival. One hundred years later, their descendants control a conglomerate with operations in 21 countries, roughly 14,000 convenience stores across Thailand, and the country's largest mobile network.

That company is CP Group, short for Charoen Pokphand, which translates roughly as "progressive agriculture." Most people outside Southeast Asia have never heard of it. That obscurity is not an accident.

The founding generation sold seeds. The second generation, led by Dhanin Chearavanont, understood that seeds were just the entry point. You could sell the seeds to the farmer, then sell the farmer the feed for his livestock, then buy back the animals for processing, then sell the packaged product to the retailer. The margin was not in any single step. It was in controlling every step.

That logic became CP Foods, which by the early 2020s was among Asia's top three poultry producers, supplying quick-service restaurant chains across Asia. The company's name appears on no consumer label in those supply chains. You have very likely eaten CP Group's protein without knowing it.

In 1978, when China began opening to foreign investment, one of the first companies the government invited in was CP Group. Dhanin Chearavanont arrived in Shenzhen before the special economic zone had roads. According to CP Group's corporate history and contemporaneous accounts from the period, the company received one of the first foreign investment certificates issued in China under the reform framework. By the early 1990s, it was operating as China's largest foreign investor by deployed capital.

That China relationship brought both scale and political complexity. CP Group's ties to the People's Republic made it simultaneously the most globally connected and the most carefully positioned conglomerate in Thailand. Its senior executives do not give many press interviews.

The Thailand-facing play was just as deliberate. In 1989, CP Group's subsidiary CP All secured the exclusive franchise to operate 7-Eleven convenience stores in Thailand. At the time, the network was small. What followed was one of the most consistent retail buildouts in Southeast Asian commercial history.

By 2023, CP All operated roughly 14,000 7-Eleven locations across Thailand. Its SET-listed annual filings reported over 14 million transactions per day across the network. That is one store for approximately every 5,000 people in the country. These locations are not just retail. They are embedded into how workers receive paychecks, pay utility bills, and access basic financial services. In Thailand, 7-Eleven is infrastructure.

CP Group was not finished.

In December 2020, as the pandemic reshaped global retail balance sheets, CP Group paid approximately 10.6 billion US dollars to acquire Tesco's hypermarket operations in Thailand and Malaysia. Tesco's press release at the time confirmed "agreement to sell its businesses in Thailand and Malaysia" for total consideration of approximately £8.2 billion. CP Group, flush with agri-food cash flows, paid while competitors could not. The deal brought roughly 2,000 hypermarket and superstore locations under CP control.

The acquisition, rebranded as Lotus's, positioned CP Group at the intersection of fresh produce, packaged goods, and everyday household retail. Combined with its food-processing operations upstream, the company now controlled product origination, wholesale supply, and end-consumer sale within a single structure.

Then, in 2023, True Corporation, a CP Group entity, completed its merger with DTAC, the Norwegian-owned mobile operator. The combined network serves approximately 52 million subscribers in Thailand. For the first time, a single Thai conglomerate controlled convenience retail at national scale, the national food-supply chain at the production end, and a major mobile network.

Read that sequence again. One ownership structure has visibility into what a Thai consumer eats, where they buy staples, and which network connects their device.

The question analysts rarely ask is why this architecture receives so little attention outside the region. CP All and True are publicly traded on the Stock Exchange of Thailand with fully audited filings. The conglomerate structure is not unusual for Asia. What is unusual is the scale of the vertical coverage and how rarely that coverage is mapped against what Amazon, Alibaba, and their Western equivalents have spent a decade trying to assemble in developed markets.

The Forbes 2024 wealth rankings estimated Dhanin Chearavanont's net worth at approximately 12.5 billion US dollars. That number is a financial metric. It does not capture the embedded commercial position that the businesses represent.

The seed shop in Yaowarat is now a historical reference in CP Group's corporate materials. The structure it founded controls a larger share of daily Thai commercial life than any other single entity, domestic or foreign. That is not an accident either. It is a century of deliberate decisions about which step in the value chain to own next.
