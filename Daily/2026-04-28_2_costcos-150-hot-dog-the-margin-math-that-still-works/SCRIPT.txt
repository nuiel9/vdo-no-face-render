In 1985, a Costco warehouse in Seattle, Washington, listed a hot dog and a twenty-ounce soda for one dollar and fifty cents. That was forty-one years ago. The price today is still one dollar and fifty cents.

In the same period, the US Consumer Price Index rose more than three hundred percent. Costco's price moved zero. That is not an oversight. It has survived three CEOs, two financial crises, a pandemic, and at least one serious internal proposal to raise it to a dollar seventy-five.

Here is the number that reframes everything. In fiscal year 2023, Costco reported four point five eight billion dollars in membership fee revenue against six point two nine billion in net income. Membership fees covered nearly seventy-three percent of net income. The bulk goods, the electronics, the giant muffins — those are not the business. The annual fee is the business. Everything else exists to justify renewing it.

When Craig Jelinek became CEO in 2012, co-founder Jim Sinegal passed the food court to him with one instruction. According to Jelinek, recounting the exchange in multiple public interviews, Sinegal's words were direct: "If you raise the price of the f***ing hot dog, I will kill you." Jelinek has not raised the price.

In 2009, input costs rose and Costco's hot dog supplier, Hebrew National, carried kosher certification requirements that imposed cost floors no negotiation could clear. Rather than raise the price, Costco dropped Hebrew National and launched its own Kirkland Signature beef hot dogs. To ensure supply certainty, the company built a hot dog manufacturing facility in California capable of producing approximately one hundred million units annually. A company constructed a factory to defend a one dollar and fifty cent price point. That decision only makes sense if you are not pricing the hot dog. You are pricing the promise it represents.

Costco's US and Canada membership renewal rate has exceeded ninety percent every year since at least 2012. That figure is disclosed in every annual 10-K. Over seventy million paid members. Ninety-two point seven percent of them renew. But here is what most case studies miss about that number.

The ninety percent renewal rate is a result. The question is what drives it. And the answer is not that Costco has better products. Sam's Club, BJ's Wholesale, and Costco sell overlapping SKUs from the same global suppliers. The answer is that Costco has planted durable memory anchors inside the warehouse experience, and the most effective one costs one dollar and fifty cents.

When a Costco member orders a hot dog, they are not just buying lunch. They are resetting their internal benchmark for fair pricing. That benchmark travels with them through the electronics aisle, the apparel section, and the pharmacy counter. The hot dog does not make those purchases cheaper. It makes them feel cheaper.

Costco's CFO Richard Galanti described food court pricing in earnings calls over multiple years as a deliberate below-market policy, held for member value signaling. The food court is not managed for profit. It is managed for perception. Management has also referenced internal research linking food court usage to higher per-visit basket size — members who stop at the food court spend more per trip than those who do not.

Sam's Club ran a matching one dollar and fifty cent hot dog in select markets. It did not produce the same renewal elasticity. BJ's Wholesale has maintained competitive food court pricing but never committed to a nominal freeze. Neither has a renewal rate above ninety percent. The hot dog alone cannot generate that outcome. But in a context where membership identity is already strong, the hot dog becomes a retrieval cue: when the renewal email arrives, the member recalls the hot dog, not the annual fee.

In 2018, according to Wall Street Journal reporting and management commentary, Costco considered raising the hot dog price to a dollar seventy-five. The proposal did not advance. The institutional memory of Sinegal's mandate outweighed the margin improvement. Twenty-five cents times one hundred million units is twenty-five million dollars. Costco decided the membership signal was worth more. The 10-K supports that decision: a one percent gain in renewal rate on seventy million members at ninety-seven dollars average fee is sixty-eight million dollars in incremental revenue.

I was wrong about this for years. I assumed the hot dog was a loss-leader promotion, like a coupon. It is not. The price is constitutional. Encoded in management culture, passed orally from CEO to CEO, backed by a California factory. You do not build a factory for a promotion.

The lesson is not about hot dogs. It is about what a company chooses to make sacred. Costco chose a price, built infrastructure to defend it, and turned it into the first number every member recalls at renewal time. The primary source is the Form 10-K, free on SEC EDGAR.
