# REVIEW: The Pentagon Office That Edits Hollywood's Scripts
**Row #46 | Hidden Truth | business-pm-pentagon-elo | Target: ~11 min | Date: 2026-04-29**

---

## Thesis

For more than six decades, the U.S. Department of Defense has operated a small office whose job is to trade hardware for screen time. The deal is not censorship: it is a procurement contract. Hollywood gets discounted access to aircraft carriers, fighter jets, and military bases worth hundreds of millions of dollars in production value. The DoD gets script approval, on-screen branding, and a recurring recruitment advertising surface that bypasses normal ad-buy budgets entirely. The office is documented in a publicly available DoD Instruction, staffed by named civil servants, and has left fingerprints on more than 800 films, per FOIA-obtained production agreements. The mainstream framing of this story, "Pentagon censors Hollywood," is the wrong frame. The correct frame is: two large institutions found a mutually beneficial procurement arrangement and shook hands on it for 75 years.

## Primary Sources

1. **DoDI 5410.16** (DoD Instruction 5410.16, "Assistance to Non-Government, Entertainment-Oriented Motion Picture, Television, and Other Production Projects," most recent revision September 2018): The governing directive. Sets the criteria for assistance, including that productions must portray the military "in a realistic, dignified, and accurate manner." Publicly available at the DoD Issuances portal.
   URL: https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/541016p.pdf

2. **"National Security Cinema" by Tom Secker and Matthew Alford (2017)**: The definitive academic study based on more than 4,000 pages of FOIA-obtained documents, cataloguing DoD involvement in 800+ films and 1,000+ TV productions. Secker runs the research archive at trove.nls.uk (FOIA file releases) and ClandesTime podcast.
   URL: https://www.plutobooks.com/9780745399478/national-security-cinema/

3. **Phil Strub interview record and film credit archive**: Phil Strub served as DoD Special Assistant for Entertainment Media from 1989 to 2018 (29 years). His name appears in the production credits of dozens of major studio films as military technical consultant and DoD liaison. On-record interviews with Strub in trade press (The Hollywood Reporter, Variety archive) confirm the voluntary, contract-based nature of the assistance.
   URL: https://variety.com/2018/film/news/phil-strub-pentagon-hollywood-liaison-retires-1202791764/

## Contrarian Angles (5 candidates, 1 selected)

**Angle A**: The office is a procurement desk, not a censorship bureau, and most of the "censored" moments were requests the studios agreed to voluntarily in exchange for hardware worth more than the changes cost.
- Source: DoDI 5410.16 + Secker/Alford production agreements (FOIA)
- Why mainstream is wrong: "Censorship" implies coercion. The FOIA documents show studios negotiated, sometimes pushed back, and in several cases walked away. The relationship is contractual.

**Angle B**: Films that were DENIED DoD assistance (The Hurt Locker, Apocalypse Now, Full Metal Jacket) won more awards per title than the assisted group, undermining the claim that assistance improves product quality.
- Source: Academy Award records vs. Secker/Alford assistance list
- Why mainstream is wrong: The "DoD makes movies better" framing ignores that the biggest critical hits often rejected the deal.

**Angle C**: The famous "500% Navy recruiting boost" statistic attributed to Top Gun (1986) has no verified primary source and has been repeatedly debunked, yet it is cited in virtually every piece about Pentagon-Hollywood as if it were fact.
- Source: Secker/Alford address this directly; original stat is traced to anecdotal Navy recruiter testimony, not a study.
- Why mainstream is wrong: The entire ROI argument for the DoD rests on an unverifiable stat.

**Angle D**: Phil Strub's 29-year tenure (1989-2018) represents a single person's editorial taste filtering more than two decades of Hollywood military portrayals. This is not policy, it is one civil servant's judgment institutionalized.
- Source: Strub interviews + Secker/Alford timeline
- Why mainstream is wrong: Critics frame this as "the Pentagon," but it was largely one man's interpretation of DoDI 5410.16.

**Angle E**: The streaming era is reducing the Pentagon's leverage, because CGI now costs less than real hardware logistics, meaning the DoD's primary negotiating chip (hardware access) is depreciating.
- Source: Visible trend in recent blockbusters (no carriers needed when you can render them)
- Why mainstream is wrong: The standard framing treats this as a stable, ongoing relationship, when the economics are shifting.

**SELECTED: Angle A** (Procurement not censorship). Rationale: Hidden Truth template demands a reframe of the dominant narrative with documentary evidence. The FOIA-backed procurement-desk angle is the most defensible, the most counterintuitive to a general audience, and the most directly supported by DoDI 5410.16. Angles C and D are folded in as supporting beats.

## 6 Lesser-Known Facts with Citations

1. **DoDI 5410.16 is publicly available.** The DoD's governing instruction for entertainment assistance has been declassified and posted publicly since its 1988 first issue. Most coverage talks about the office as if its rules are secret; the document is 8 pages and freely downloadable from the DoD Issuances portal. (Source: DoDI 5410.16, September 2018 revision)

2. **Phil Strub held the position for 29 years.** From 1989 to 2018, a single civil servant named Phil Strub was the primary filter between Hollywood script submissions and DoD approval decisions. His tenure spans from "Black Rain" (1989) to "Top Gun: Maverick" (pre-production approval, which he signed off before retiring). (Source: Variety obituary for his retirement, 2018)

3. **The FOIA archive covers 800+ films and 1,000+ TV shows.** Tom Secker and Matthew Alford's FOIA requests, processed over several years, produced production agreements, script notes, and correspondence for a documented list that dwarfs what most people assume. The list includes obvious entries (Iron Man, Transformers) and surprising ones (reality TV and daytime programming). (Source: Secker/Alford, "National Security Cinema," 2017)

4. **"The Hurt Locker" was made without DoD assistance.** Director Kathryn Bigelow and writer Mark Boal chose not to seek DoD support, which gave them full creative freedom over the script. The film won 6 Academy Awards including Best Picture and Best Director. Films with DoD assistance have won fewer per-title. (Source: Bigelow interviews, 2009-2010; Academy Award records)

5. **Top Gun was filmed at Naval Air Station Miramar.** The 1986 Paramount production used NAS Miramar in San Diego as the primary filming location, with Navy F-14 Tomcat jets and carrier access under a formal production support agreement. The deal required the Navy to review and approve marketing materials as well as the script itself. The agreement is in the FOIA-obtained production files documented by Secker/Alford. (Source: Secker/Alford FOIA archive + NAS Miramar production records)

6. **GI Jane (1997) was initially denied DoD assistance.** The DoD objected to a subplot involving sexual assault in the script. After negotiations, the film was made largely without DoD support. This case is documented in the Secker/Alford archive and illustrates both the DoD's criteria and a studio's decision to walk away from the procurement deal rather than make the requested changes. (Source: Secker/Alford FOIA documents; reported in their 2017 book)
