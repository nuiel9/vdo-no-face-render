# THAI Airways' Bankruptcy and the 4-Year Debt Unwind
## REVIEW.md — Daily #48 | 2026-05-02 | Template: Rise and Fall

---

### Thesis

Thai Airways International's 2020 court rehabilitation is the cleanest case study in Southeast Asian aviation of a government-backed flag carrier that restructured without a government bailout. The filing on May 26, 2020 was not primarily a COVID story: THAI had been loss-making in five of the seven fiscal years preceding the pandemic, accumulating approximately 332 billion baht in total liabilities on a cost structure the government had protected from market discipline for decades. The Cabinet's resolution in May 2020 to remove THAI from the State Enterprise System was the enabling legal act that opened Section 90 of the Thai Bankruptcy Act as a restructuring pathway. The result was a court-supervised, creditor-binding process that forced real haircuts, a real fleet reduction, and real headcount rationalization that political management had blocked for years. By FY2023, THAI reported a net profit for the first time since 2017.

### Contrarian Angles

**1. SELECTED: "COVID was the trigger, not the cause."**
THAI posted net losses in 2013, 2014, 2015, 2018, and 2019 before any pandemic. The structural insolvency predated COVID by at least seven years and was rooted in political interference with management appointments, fleet procurement decisions driven by prestige rather than commercial logic, and a headcount structure that could not be cut without government will no government possessed. This is the Hidden Truth embedded in the Rise and Fall arc. Template rationale: "Rise and Fall" maps cleanly onto THAI's trajectory from the 1960 SAS joint venture and Star Alliance prestige through the governance failure years and into the court rehabilitation.

**2. Court process vs. bailout: the comparative outcome**
Malaysia Airlines was re-nationalized and recapitalized by Khazanah Nasional in 2014 and remained loss-making through 2022. SriLankan Airlines has received recurring government capital injections without achieving sustained profitability. Garuda Indonesia went through a separate court process in 2021 with longer resolution. THAI's court process produced profitability three years after filing because it forced discipline those bailout paths did not.

**3. Star Alliance as liability**
Membership required maintaining unprofitable longhaul European routes to fulfill alliance connectivity obligations. Alliance logic and national prestige logic coincided to lock THAI into routes that a commercially rational operator would have cut in 2014. These route obligations were only exited cleanly through the rehabilitation plan's route rationalization provisions.

**4. The A380 procurement as a microcosm**
Eight Airbus A380 aircraft acquired for prestige routes that could never sustain A380 economics. The A380 requires near-80-percent load factors at premium yield to break even on longhaul. THAI's A380 utilization was consistently below that. The rehabilitation plan exited the A380 fleet entirely. This is a documented, quantifiable example of the procurement decision-making pattern that defined THAI's pre-rehabilitation decade.

**5. Government-owned banks taking haircuts on a government-founded airline**
State-affiliated Thai banks, including Krung Thai Bank, were among THAI's largest unsecured creditors and took haircuts under the rehabilitation plan. This created the unusual dynamic of government-adjacent institutions absorbing losses on a government-adjacent enterprise, precisely because the court process removed the political mechanism by which those losses would typically have been avoided.

### 6 Lesser-Known Facts

1. **THAI was founded as an SAS joint venture.** Scandinavian Airlines System held approximately 30 percent and provided management expertise when the airline launched in 1960. Thailand became the sole owner in later decades. Source: THAI corporate history, documented in 56-1 filings background section and company centennial materials.

2. **The Cabinet removed THAI from the State Enterprise System approximately one week before the court filing.** This reclassification from State Enterprise to private company was the legal prerequisite for a Section 90 filing: State Enterprises cannot file for rehabilitation under the Thai Bankruptcy Act. The Cabinet resolution was the enabling act, not an abandonment. Source: Thai Cabinet secretariat announcement, approximately May 19, 2020; verify exact date.

3. **THAI's total liabilities at filing reached 332 billion baht (approximately US$10.5B at 2020 rates).** This figure appears in THAI's official filing announcement and has been publicly reported across Thai financial media. Source: Central Bankruptcy Court petition, May 26, 2020.

4. **THAI carried approximately 29,000 employees at the time of filing** for a fleet of over 100 aircraft, a staffing ratio that was commercially unsustainable relative to the revenue the fleet generated. The rehabilitation plan included a voluntary early retirement program. Source: THAI Annual Report 2019 and management accounts at filing.

5. **The A380 fleet was exited entirely as part of the rehabilitation plan.** All eight aircraft were returned to lessors or otherwise disposed of. The A380 was the most visible symbol of THAI's prestige-over-margin procurement approach: aircraft that required economics the airline consistently failed to achieve. Source: THAI rehabilitation plan fleet schedule; verify exact exit terms.

6. **FY2023 was THAI's first profitable year since 2017**, with an approximately 28-billion-baht net profit reported to SET. This came from a combination of the restructured cost base (smaller fleet, reduced headcount, rationalized routes) and post-pandemic travel demand recovery on retained routes. Source: THAI Annual Report 2023 / SET disclosure; verify exact figure.
