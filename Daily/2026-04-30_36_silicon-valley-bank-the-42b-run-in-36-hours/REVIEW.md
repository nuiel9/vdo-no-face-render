## Thesis

Silicon Valley Bank's collapse in March 2023 is told as a story of a Twitter panic and a VC herd stampede. That story is not wrong. It is incomplete. Three structural failures preexisted the run: a bond portfolio that was functionally insolvent under mark-to-market accounting ($91.3B in held-to-maturity securities carrying $15.1B in unrealized losses as of December 31, 2022), a board-level risk governance vacuum (no Chief Risk Officer for eight months of the fastest rate-hiking cycle in 40 years), and a regulatory exemption the bank's own executives had lobbied for that specifically removed the requirement for enhanced interest rate stress testing. The $42 billion withdrawal on March 9, 2023, was the match. The fuel had been accumulating for three years.

## Contrarian Angles

1. **SELECTED: "The deregulation short-cut"** — SVB was among the mid-size bank executives who publicly supported S. 2155 (2018), which raised the enhanced prudential standards threshold from $50B to $250B. As a direct result, SVB was not subject to DFAST interest rate stress testing in the years its portfolio duration risk was compounding. The very risk that destroyed it was the risk regulators were no longer required to formally test. Template fit: News is exactly right — this is regulatory forensics with legislative record as primary source. Most YouTube coverage of SVB ignores the lobbying angle entirely.

2. **"The CRO vacancy"** — SVB had no Chief Risk Officer from April 2022 through January 2023. The Federal Reserve raised rates 425 basis points during that window. No internal executive was formally accountable for duration risk as it built. This is documented in the FDIC and Barr post-mortems. Strong secondary angle if the deregulation story is too legislative for the hook.

3. **"The 93% problem"** — The FDIC insures up to $250K per depositor. SVB's deposit base was 93.8% uninsured. Every rational business depositor had an incentive to leave even before any panic started. The social media explanation treats the run as irrational; the insurance structure explains it as coldly rational. Requires the 10-K deposit schedule for sourcing.

4. **"Founders Fund as catalyst"** — Reporting by The Information and Bloomberg attributed early withdrawal instructions from Peter Thiel's Founders Fund to portfolio companies on March 9, 2023. This is the network-effect angle: a single VC's advice seeded a Slack-and-GroupChat cascade. Needs clear sourcing — cite original journalism with attribution rather than stating it as fact.

5. **"The Fed's missed findings"** — The Federal Reserve Bank of San Francisco issued multiple supervisory findings (MRAs and MRIAs) to SVB in 2021-2023, documented in the Barr report. SVB did not fully remediate them. The regulator most responsible for catching this problem also missed it. Human verifier should confirm the count and timing from the Barr report before citing any specific number.

2. As of December 31, 2022, approximately 93.8% of SVB's deposits exceeded the $250,000 FDIC insurance limit. **VERIFY exact percentage in SVB 2022 10-K deposit footnote.** Means nearly all depositors had rational incentive to leave regardless of panic dynamics.

3. SVB's held-to-maturity securities portfolio carried $15.1 billion in unrealized losses at year-end 2022. Under US GAAP, HTM unrealized losses do not flow through equity or income. They are disclosed in footnotes only. **VERIFY in SVB 2022 10-K investment securities note.** The losses were public; they were not prominent.

4. SVB CEO Greg Becker sold approximately $3.6 million in company stock on February 27, 2023, under a 10b5-1 plan, roughly 10 days before the FDIC seizure. **VERIFY exact amount and date on SEC Form 4 (EDGAR, search Becker + SIVB).** The 10b5-1 plan means the sale was pre-scheduled, not a real-time read of the situation — that nuance matters for editorial fairness.

5. The Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act (S. 2155), signed May 24, 2018, raised the systemically important financial institution (SIFI) threshold from $50 billion to $250 billion in assets. SVB had approximately $55 billion in assets at year-end 2017. **VERIFY SVB's asset size at the time of passage from 2017 or 2018 10-K.** The law was tailor-made for banks of exactly SVB's size.

6. First Citizens BancShares acquired SVB's deposits and loans from the FDIC on March 27, 2023. The FDIC estimated the transaction would cost the Deposit Insurance Fund approximately $20 billion. **VERIFY DIF cost estimate in FDIC press release PR-22-2023 or subsequent DIF disclosures.**
