## Description (100 words)

On March 8, 2023, Silicon Valley Bank filed an SEC disclosure that turned into a starting gun. By the next afternoon, depositors had submitted $42 billion in withdrawal requests. Within 36 hours, the 16th-largest bank in the United States was seized by regulators.

The social media panic explanation is real. It is also incomplete. This video traces the three structural failures that made the run possible: a $91 billion bond portfolio with $15 billion in hidden losses, a Chief Risk Officer vacancy lasting eight months, and a regulatory exemption the bank had lobbied for that removed formal interest rate stress testing.

## Pinned Comment Draft

Three things I found in the primary documents that most coverage skips:

1. SVB's 2022 annual report (10-K, filed Feb 24, 2023) disclosed $15.1 billion in unrealized losses on its held-to-maturity portfolio. Those losses are not required to show up on the income statement under US GAAP accounting rules. They were public. They were in the footnotes. They were not prominent.

2. SVB had no Chief Risk Officer from April 2022 until January 2023, a period during which the Federal Reserve raised rates 425 basis points.

3. The Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act (S. 2155, 2018) specifically exempted banks under $250 billion in assets from the enhanced stress testing requirements that might have caught this. SVB had lobbied for that exemption.

The run was the mechanism. The insolvency was already real before the first wire transfer was submitted. Sources in the description.
