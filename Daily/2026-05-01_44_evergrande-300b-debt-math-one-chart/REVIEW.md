## Thesis

China Evergrande Group did not collapse because of opaque or reckless borrowing that nobody saw. Its liabilities were disclosed in public exchange filings for years. The collapse was triggered by a specific policy intervention: Beijing's "three red lines" directive of August 2020, which reclassified debt thresholds for every major property developer and cut off Evergrande's ability to refinance. The $300 billion figure in the title is a symptom. The cause was a deliberate government deleveraging mandate applied to a company structurally incapable of meeting its new constraints without an extended runway. This video argues that Evergrande was not a hidden time bomb. It was a known and disclosed risk that the government chose to detonate on a specific policy schedule.

## 5 Contrarian Angles

**Angle 1 [SELECTED]: Policy-triggered collapse, not organic market failure.**
Mainstream narrative: Evergrande borrowed irresponsibly, the bubble burst, and the market discovered the risk. Our thesis: Beijing issued a specific directive in August 2020 setting three debt ratios. Evergrande breached all three. The policy explicitly barred "red" companies from increasing total debt. The collapse was not a market discovery. It was a government-imposed constraint on a business model that depended on perpetual refinancing. The question is not why Evergrande failed but why Beijing chose to remove the financial oxygen at that speed and in that sequence.

Template rationale: Pitch Deck template fits precisely because the story turns on three specific numbers (the red line ratios), one exchange filing (H1 2021 total liabilities), and one structural chart (presales vs. completions gap). Slide-like data visuals and ratio breakdowns are the natural vocabulary for this template. The "debt math in one chart" title earns its promise only if we show the math explicitly.

**Angle 2: Retail homebuyers as the real political forcing function.**
The June 2022 mortgage boycott by retail buyers, not the December 2021 offshore bond default, was what changed the speed of Beijing's project-completion mandates. Offshore institutional creditors had no political leverage inside China. One million-plus domestic retail mortgagors did. The sequencing matters: the offshore market took the loss first; the political response moved only when domestic buyers organized.

**Angle 3: The moral hazard precedent.**
By protecting retail buyers while allowing offshore bondholders to absorb losses, Beijing established a template for future Chinese developer insolvencies. This effectively told the offshore bond market what the priority order would be in any future restructuring: mainland retail creditors first, offshore institutional last. Every subsequent Chinese developer bond was priced in the shadow of this precedent.

**Angle 4: The GDP concentration story.**
Evergrande is one node in a simultaneous contraction of a sector that represented 25 to 29 percent of Chinese GDP. The Evergrande media narrative displaced coverage of the broader rebalancing, which is the real macroeconomic event. The $300 billion figure is attention-grabbing but smaller than the aggregate liabilities of all defaulting developers combined by 2023.

**Angle 5: The jurisdictional fiction exposed by liquidation.**
Offshore bonds existed because international investors were willing to lend against structures where mainland assets were legally inaccessible to them in a default. Every bond prospectus flagged this as a risk factor. The January 2024 Hong Kong liquidation order made visible what the risk disclosures had said for years: offshore creditors had no real enforcement path into the mainland asset pool. The legal structure was always the risk.

## 6 Lesser-Known Facts with Citations

1. The three red lines were not a public regulation or published law. They were an internal regulatory memo distributed to major developers in August 2020. Public confirmation emerged through developer disclosures in subsequent months. (Source: Caixin Global reporting September-October 2020; PBOC Financial Stability Report 2021. VERIFY via PBOC report text and Caixin archive.)

2. Evergrande was operating in more than 280 cities across China at its peak, not just its Guangzhou headquarters and major Tier 1 markets. Most public coverage focused on the headline cities; the geographic exposure was far broader. (Source: Evergrande 2020 Annual Report, HK Exchange. VERIFY exact city count from the annual filing.)

3. Forbes named Xu Jiayin China's wealthiest individual in 2017. By 2022 and into 2023, Bloomberg Billionaires Index and Hurun tracking showed his estimated personal wealth had declined more than 90 percent from that peak. Evergrande's equity collapse was one of the fastest wealth destructions in modern corporate history for a single founder. (Source: Forbes China Rich List 2017; Bloomberg Billionaires Index. VERIFY peak year, peak figure, and decline percentage from current tracking data.)

4. The June-July 2022 mortgage boycott extended well beyond Evergrande to dozens of developers and reportedly involved buyers coordinating refusals across more than 300 project sites across China at its peak. The coordination occurred partly through social media platforms. (Source: Bloomberg July 2022; Reuters July 2022. VERIFY the count of project sites and confirm the action was not exclusively Evergrande-associated.)

5. The creditor who filed the Hong Kong liquidation petition (HCCW 179/2022) was Top Shine Global Limited, an offshore bondholder holding a reported approximately $862 million in Evergrande offshore bonds. Top Shine had been pursuing restructuring through the court process since June 2022. (Source: Media coverage and court filings. VERIFY exact entity name, bond amount, and petition filing date via judiciary.hk or Bloomberg Law.)

6. The three red line ratios were not novel financial metrics invented for Evergrande. They were standard accounting ratios that Chinese regulators repurposed as mandatory policy thresholds with automatic consequences: a "red" classification meant zero new debt permitted, with no grace period. The innovation was the enforcement mechanism, not the metrics. (Source: PBOC Financial Stability Report 2021; academic analysis of Chinese property sector regulation.
