In August 2020, four of China's financial regulators distributed a memo reclassifying how every major property developer could borrow. The memo set three debt ratios. China Evergrande Group, listed on the Hong Kong Stock Exchange under ticker 3333, was in breach of all three at once. Its own half-year exchange filings, lodged with the HKEX in August 2021, showed total liabilities above $300 billion. The company had collected deposits from over 1.5 million buyers for apartments it had not yet finished building. None of that was secret. All of it was in the public filings. The question is why it took the world so long to read them.

Xu Jiayin founded Evergrande in Guangzhou in 1996 with a single aim: move as fast as the land market would allow. China's urban expansion in the late 1990s and 2000s created a nearly frictionless environment for a developer willing to use presales as its core financing instrument. Evergrande would acquire land, design a project, and begin selling units to buyers before a single foundation was poured. The cash from those sales funded construction. Then it would acquire more land and repeat.

This was not unusual in Chinese property development. But Evergrande took it further than any other firm. By 2020, the company was operating in more than 280 cities, with a portfolio spanning residential towers, theme parks, electric vehicles, and a professional soccer club. Each new acquisition was funded partly by presale cash and partly by offshore and onshore debt. The model worked as long as land prices kept rising and buyers kept buying. The three red lines ended both assumptions simultaneously.

The three ratios were: a liability-to-asset ratio below 70 percent, excluding presale receipts; a net debt-to-equity ratio below 100 percent; and a cash-to-short-term-debt ratio above one. A developer breaching all three was classified as "red" and could not increase its total debt at all. Evergrande was red. That meant the company could not borrow its way through the next land cycle. It had to sell down inventory, collect receivables, and service existing debt from operating cash flow. For a company built around perpetual debt expansion, this was not a slowdown. It was a structural stop.

The $300 billion figure in the H1 2021 half-year results is worth reading carefully. That number represents total liabilities, which includes trade payables, construction contractor obligations, and presale deposits collected from buyers who had not yet received their apartments. The gross debt figure, meaning bank loans plus bonds, was smaller. The offshore bond book, denominated primarily in US dollars, was the tranche most visible to international investors. Those bonds had traded at or near par as recently as early 2021.

By September 2021, Evergrande had missed coupon payments on multiple US dollar bonds. Management released a statement saying the company was experiencing, in their words, "unprecedented difficulties." Coupon payments carried 30-day grace periods under bond indenture terms. Those grace periods expired through October and November. The company engaged restructuring advisers. Suppliers stopped delivering materials to construction sites across the country.

The political pressure did not come from the offshore bondholders, who were primarily institutional investors with no domestic political standing. It came from the retail buyers inside China who had paid deposits for apartments that existed only on floor plans. These were Chinese households, many of whom had taken out mortgages on unbuilt units. In mid-2022, homebuyers across dozens of stalled projects began a coordinated mortgage boycott, refusing to pay installments on loans for homes that had never been delivered. Lenders were holding mortgages with no collateral behind them.

The Chinese banking system carried roughly $5.2 trillion in mortgage loans outstanding at that point. Evergrande was not the only developer with stalled sites. Country Garden, Sunac, and Kaisa had all disclosed their own liquidity strains by mid-2022. Beijing had structured the three red lines to force a sector-wide deleveraging. What the policy had not fully modeled was the speed at which that deleveraging would put the entire real estate sector's contribution to GDP at risk.

Evergrande formally missed the 30-day grace period on its offshore bonds in December 2021. Rating agencies classified the company as in selective default. This was the largest offshore bond default by a Chinese corporate entity up to that point by notional amount. The company's Hong Kong-listed shares had fallen more than 80 percent from their 2020 peak by year-end 2021.

Beijing's response was deliberate and calibrated. The government did not provide a direct bailout. State media ran editorials reminding readers that housing is for living in, not for speculation, a phrase drawn from the Communist Party's 2017 National Congress resolutions. Local governments in cities where Evergrande had significant stalled inventory were instructed to monitor the situation and facilitate completion of pre-sold projects where practicable. The message to markets was clear: equity holders and offshore bondholders would bear losses. Retail buyers inside China would be protected to the extent the government could manage it.

The contagion moved quickly. Within 18 months of Evergrande's default, several other large developers had also defaulted or sought restructuring. Country Garden, which had carried investment-grade credit ratings as recently as 2022, disclosed a liquidity shortfall in 2023 and missed coupon payments. Aggregate liabilities across defaulted Chinese developers exceeded $500 billion by mid-2023. This was no longer an Evergrande problem. It was a managed sector restructuring at a scale that had no modern precedent in China.

Chinese real estate and related industries had accounted for roughly 25 to 29 percent of GDP by most measurement methodologies, according to estimates from the International Monetary Fund and Goldman Sachs Research. A simultaneous contraction across dozens of developers produced a deflationary impulse that moved through the consumer sector and the local government fiscal system. Land sales revenue, which had funded a significant portion of municipal budgets, fell sharply. Local governments that had used land sales to finance infrastructure found their fiscal capacity reduced well below historical levels.

A creditor group filed a liquidation petition against Evergrande in the Hong Kong High Court. The case is numbered HCCW 179/2022. It was heard across multiple sessions through 2022 and 2023 as the company submitted successive restructuring proposals. Each proposal failed to satisfy the court that offshore creditors would receive adequate recovery. On January 29, 2024, the Hong Kong High Court issued a winding-up order. Liquidators were appointed. Evergrande was legally wound down.

The liquidators immediately confronted a problem that debt analysts had flagged in deal documents for years. Evergrande's primary assets, its land banks, unfinished towers, and completed inventory, were held in mainland Chinese subsidiaries governed by Chinese law. A Hong Kong winding-up order does not automatically grant liquidators control over mainland assets. The liquidators had to seek recognition from mainland courts, a process with no guaranteed timeline. Offshore bondholders were looking at recoveries measured in cents on the dollar, and the legal path to those recoveries was not clear.

The single chart that explains the Evergrande collapse is not a graph of the company's total debt. It is a graph of China's urban housing presales versus housing completions from 2015 to 2021. By 2020, the gap between units sold and units completed had widened to tens of millions of square meters. Every major developer was running the same gap. The three red lines closed the financing channel that had kept that gap from becoming visible. The debt was the symptom. The gap between what was sold and what was built was the structural failure.

Here is what the mainstream narrative gets wrong about Evergrande. The company did not collapse because investors suddenly lost confidence in a poorly managed firm. It collapsed because a deliberate government policy removed the specific financial instrument, perpetual debt refinancing, that had kept the firm operational. The question is not why Evergrande failed. The question is why Beijing chose to impose those constraints at that speed and in that sequence. The answer, visible in the three red lines policy documents and the subsequent guidance to local governments, is that the Chinese government had decided a managed deflation of the property sector was less dangerous than continued expansion. Evergrande was the largest test case for that judgment. Chinese residential property prices in major cities remain well below their 2020 peaks. The mortgage boycott of 2022 did not trigger a systemic banking crisis. The 1.5 million unfinished apartments are still, as of this recording, only partially delivered. Whether the judgment was correct is a calculation governments make once, under pressure, with incomplete data. The three red lines memo was that calculation written down in advance.
