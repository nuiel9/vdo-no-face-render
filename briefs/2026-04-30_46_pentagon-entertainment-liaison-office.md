## The angle (Hidden Truth)

Frame this as a **services-procurement story**, not a censorship story. Studios buy Pentagon hardware (jets, carriers, bases, troops as extras) at a fraction of commercial cost. The price is editorial: the DoD reviews scripts and negotiates changes before signing the production assistance agreement. That's not conspiracy — it's a documented contract. The "hidden truth" is the **margin**: at near-zero marginal cost to the DoD (the hardware exists, the personnel are paid anyway), studios save tens of millions, and in exchange the Pentagon gets the most effective recruiting and PR vehicle in the world. Both sides win. The audience whose perception is shaped is the third party, not in the deal.

## Anchor facts (primary-source-locked)

Each fact below is verifiable. Prompt 3 should pull from these; the routine should NOT introduce specific dates, dollar figures, or named officers without checking against this list.

1. **Office name:** Department of Defense Entertainment Media Office (sometimes "DoD Film Liaison Unit"). Located in Los Angeles. Source: dodi 5410.16; Wikipedia "Military–entertainment complex".

2. **Governing policy:** **DoD Instruction 5410.16** — "DoD Assistance to Non-Government, Entertainment-Oriented Motion Picture, Television, and Video Productions." Public PDF: https://www.esd.whs.mil/Portals/54/Documents/DD/issuances/dodi/541016p.pdf

3. **Phil Strub:** Director of Entertainment Media at DoD, **1989–2018** (29 years). USC cinema-production master's, USN officer. Helped produce 100+ films. Replaced by **David Evans** in 2018.

4. **Each service has its own LA office:** Air Force, Army, Navy, Marines, Coast Guard. They negotiate separately under the 5410.16 framework. The Army's Office of the Chief of Public Affairs — West (OCPA-West) is the public-facing entity. Source: army.mil/outreach/ocpa-west-faqs.html

5. **The "Dara" database:** Strub's-era internal DoD database tracking every entertainment production that approached the department. Visual potential — abstract data viz, never render real screenshots.

6. **Top Gun (1986) deal mechanics:** Navy reviewed and approved the script before lending F-14s and the USS Enterprise / Ranger / Carl Vinson. Recruiting tables were set up in theater lobbies (LA and Detroit) when the film opened. Navy's 1986 recruiting/advertising budget: **~$31M**. The often-repeated "500% recruiting increase" stat **is debunked / unattributed** — explicitly do not use it. Source: military.com 2022, LAist 2020.

7. **Top Gun: Maverick (2022):** Same playbook, modern era — DoD again provided fleet access, again negotiated script approval. FOIA-released contract details available via Tom Secker / Spy Culture.

8. **Films the DoD refused / asked to change:** Apocalypse Now, Platoon, Independence Day (Roland Emmerich rejected DoD edits, used civilian aircraft instead). Source: David Robb, *Operation Hollywood* (2004).

9. **The FOIA evidence base:** Tom Secker (spyculture.com) + Matthew Alford published *National Security Cinema* (2017), documenting **800+ films and 1,000+ TV episodes** with traceable DoD involvement via FOIA-released production assistance agreements.

## Spielberg / Disclosure Day connection (handle with care)

The meta-hook is *that this kind of arrangement is the business behind summer blockbusters of the kind Spielberg is releasing*. **Do NOT claim** Spielberg used the DoD liaison office for *Disclosure Day* — it's a sci-fi conspiracy film, the DoD typically declines cooperation when scripts depict government cover-ups, and we have no evidence either way. **Do NOT claim** he used DoD assistance for *Close Encounters* (1977) without primary source — there are inconsistent secondary accounts. The clean angle: end the video on "every Spielberg-scale summer blockbuster involving uniforms or hardware passed through this office's review queue at some point" — that's defensible without naming a specific Spielberg film.
